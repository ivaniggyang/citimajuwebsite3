# Slide Content Guide

## The Purpose of a Presentation

A Citi Maju presentation exists to move the audience toward a decision: to award a contract, approve a scope, or gain confidence in the group. Every slide must serve that purpose. Slides that do not contribute to that goal should be cut.

---

## Slide Count

| Presentation Type | Recommended Slides |
|---|---|
| Company introduction (unsolicited) | 8–12 |
| Tender / bid presentation | 12–18 |
| Project progress update | 6–10 |
| Executive briefing | 5–8 |

Less is more. A 30-slide deck signals a lack of editorial discipline. If you cannot make your case in 15 slides, the problem is usually unclear thinking, not insufficient slides.

---

## Standard Deck Structure

### 1. Cover Slide
- Project or presentation title
- Prepared for / occasion
- Date
- Citi Maju Group lockup

### 2. Contents (optional, for decks over 12 slides)
A simple list of sections. No page numbers needed — this is a live presentation.

### 3. Who We Are (1 slide)
One paragraph, or four to six bullet points. Not a company history. State what the group does, where (Klang Valley), and lead with the most important credential (CIDB G7).

### 4. What We Do (1–2 slides)
Services, structured by entity if relevant. Use icons or a simple table — not a paragraph wall.

### 5. Why Trust Us (1 slide)
Certifications + track record. Numbers where available: metres of pipe installed, number of developments served, years of operation. No vague claims.

### 6. The Proposal / Update / Solution (variable)
The core content. Structure this around what the audience needs to know to make their decision:
- What you are proposing to do
- How you will do it (method, resources, timeline)
- What it costs (if a commercial slide is appropriate)
- What happens next

### 7. Project References (1 slide, table format)
| Project Description | Scope | Scale | Year |
|---|---|---|---|

### 8. Contact / Close
Citi Maju Group lockup + contact details + tagline: "Trusted. Certified. Delivered."

## Bullet Points — How to Write Them

Each bullet should be a standalone, complete thought. Do not write fragments.

**Good:**
- CIDB G7 Grade — Malaysia's highest contractor certification
- Pipe jacking and directional drilling for trenchless underground works
- Completed water reticulation for over 3,000 units in Klang Valley

**Bad:**
- CIDB G7
- Trenchless works
- Experience

Bullets should be parallel in structure: all noun phrases, or all verb phrases, or all full sentences — not a mix.

Maximum five bullets per slide. If you have more than five, split the slide.

## Callout Slides

For key statistics or facts that deserve emphasis, use a full-slide callout:
- Large number or short phrase in Cormorant Garamond 400, 60–72pt
- Context label in Sora 300, ALL CAPS, below in smaller type
- Background: Navy or Brand Blue

Example:
```
[72pt Garamond]  G7
[Sora, 10pt ALL CAPS]  CIDB GRADE — MALAYSIA'S HIGHEST CONTRACTOR CLASSIFICATION
```

---

## Tables

Use tables when you have structured data: pricing summaries, project references, programme overviews, scope comparisons.

- Header row: Blue Mid or Brand Blue background, white text, Sora 600 ALL CAPS
- Alternating rows: White and Blue Pale
- Keep column count to five or fewer on a single slide
- Left-align text cells, right-align number cells

---

## What Not to Include

- Mission and vision statements on their own slide (fold this into "Who We Are" if needed)
- Org charts (unless specifically requested)
- Generic stock photography labelled as Citi Maju work
- Overly complex technical drawings at slide scale — link to or distribute separately
- Slide headers that repeat the same word on every slide ("Introduction", "Overview", "Summary")

---

## Speaking Notes

Use the Notes panel in PowerPoint for speaking notes. The slide shows the key point; the notes contain the detail. Never read directly from a slide.

If the deck will be distributed without a presenter, add a short explanatory sentence or two to each slide's notes or directly on the slide — the deck must stand alone without narration.
